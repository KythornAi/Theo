## 1. Reddit ADHD Communities — Theme of the Day

### Theme: "Stop selling me systems I can't stick to"

The dominant sentiment across r/ADHD, r/adhdwomen, and r/ADHDers is **tool fatigue** — people are tired of cycling through new planners, apps, and productivity methods. The threads gaining traction are the ones validating that *fewer systems, simpler routines, and lower overhead* work better than elaborate setups.

### Notable threads

**r/ADHD (general):**
- **"What routine have you stuck with that has actually helped long term?"** — The most-upvoted approach was "start with one tiny thing" rather than overhauling everything at once. Users who succeeded long-term framed routines around momentum, not discipline.
- **"Successful people with ADHD… what actually worked for you?"** — Key takeaway: successful ADHDers reported cycling through "the new system" every 6 months. The common thread was not a specific tool but ruthlessly reducing system overhead. Google Calendar + one notebook were the most cited combo.
- **"What's the cheat code you've discovered that made everything easier?"** — Time blindness hacks dominated: timer-based anchoring, smartwatch vibration reminders, and keeping essentials (keys, meds, deodorant) in visible/duplicate locations.
- **"My ADHD turns 10 minutes into either 2 or 60, how do you anchor yourself?"** — Directly validates time blindness as the #1 pain point. The thread discussed various anchoring techniques.

**r/adhdwomen:**
- **"Using ChatGPT to schedule my day"** — A growing trend of using AI as an executive-function crutch: having ChatGPT structure the day, remember tasks, and reduce decision fatigue. This signals a market gap for tools that act as "cognitive scaffolding" vs. passive planners.
- **"I know no kind of planner ever helps an ADHDer"** — Classic frustration thread. Key insight: planners fail because they rely on the executive functions (planning, sequencing, time estimation) that are precisely what's impaired. Solutions that *replace* those functions do better than those that depend on them.

**r/ADHDers:**
- **"Has anyone actually found a good app/method for being productive?"** — The most common "what works" answers: analog calendar + digital calendar combo; visible whiteboards; making tasks smaller than you think they need to be.
- **"A rant about ADHD productivity apps"** — Strong criticism of apps that require setup, maintenance, or "learning the app before you can use it." The friction of onboarding a new tool was cited as the #1 reason people abandon them.
- **"Best Planning/Routine/Productivity Apps for AuDHD?"** — Akiflow and time-blocking methods mentioned positively. Time-blocking (as opposed to time-boxing) came up as the method that actually maps to how ADHD brains work.

**Cross-community signal:**
- **r/SideProject: "Do daily reset planners actually help productivity or just feel good?"** — Directly relevant to PNP's Daily Reset concept. The thread questions whether daily resets are genuinely useful or just produce a dopamine hit of "feeling organized" without actual follow-through. This is exactly the validation question PNP needs to answer with its product positioning.
- **r/SideProject: "I got tired of fighting to-do apps with my ADHD, so I built one around time blindness"** — Another builder went the same direction as PNP. Validates time blindness focus.

## Key Takeaway for PNP

The market is saturated with "planners for ADHD" — but almost all of them require the very executive functions they're supposed to support. The gap that's getting stronger validation every week is:

> **Not another planner. A time-blindness rescue tool for the middle of the day.**

The r/ADHD threads keep circling back to the same frustration: systems work *in the morning* and fall apart by 2pm. The daily reset concept — specifically positioned as a *catch-me-when-I've-lost-my-place* tool, not a planner — is the product the community keeps asking for but nobody is selling.
