## 1. The Body Stress Scan (Chapter 1)
App match: Gemini wave-rider body-map ("where does it land?")

Find a quiet spot. Sit or lie down, whichever feels comfortable. Close your eyes.
Start with three slow breaths, in through the nose, out through the mouth.
Bring attention to your forehead. Notice any tightness. Imagine softening it by five percent.
Move to your jaw. Is it clenched? Loosen it slightly.
Drop your shoulders by a fraction. Notice the relief.
Scan down through arms, chest, stomach, legs. Wherever you feel tension, label it ("tight," "buzzing," "numb") without judgment.
Rest your hands on your belly. Breathe slowly, as if you're sending the breath into those tense places.
This is not about eliminating stress. It's about observing where it lands in your body. By noticing, you're interrupting the automatic stress loop.
As you finish, remind yourself: "Stress is here, but so am I." Carry that awareness into your day.

## 2. The Balance Audit + Reset (Chapter 2)
App match: Gemini pocket-reset card generator (the "Balance Audit" + reset card is lifted directly from this)

Take a notebook or notes app. Draw two columns: Column A - What steadies me and Column B - What tips me off balance.
Under Column B, list the top five things that sap your attention or energy most days (e.g., doom-scrolling at night, pings during deep work, skipping lunch, saying yes automatically).
Under Column A, list five small actions that reliably steady you (e.g., a two-minute walk, water + stretch, closing the door for a call, a 60-second breath, putting the phone in another room for 10 minutes).
List what comes to mind. Then ask: what's one adjustment I can make this week to lean more into Column A?
Now add a Reset Card you can save, screenshot, or copy onto a card:
- Three breaths.
- Name the tilt ("I'm anxious," "I'm exhausted").
- One tiny steadying action.
This isn't about guilt. It's about awareness. Balance isn't found; it's built, one pause at a time.

## 3. The Gratitude Expansion (Chapter 3)
App match: Theo gratitude-reset

Tonight, try this exercise:
Write down one thing you're grateful for.
Beneath it, write three ripple effects - why it matters, what it connects to, who else benefits.
Sit quietly for one minute and notice how your body feels as you read the list.
You might find the gratitude didn't come from pretending life was perfect, but from retraining the mind to notice the parts of life that already worked.
You don't need a journal with gold-edged pages. A sticky note will do. Three specific things, one sentence each. The point isn't poetic writing, it's training attention towards what sustains you.
Gratitude doesn't erase the hard parts, but it shifts the lens so they're not the whole story.

## 4. The Self-Compassion Reset (Chapter 4) - 10 minutes
App match: recovery menu, 10-min option

This 10-minute reset can be used anytime the critic is loud.
- Notice the Critic: Write down one harsh phrase your critic uses. ("I'm not good enough.")
- Anchor in Breath: Take three rounds of in-for-4, out-for-6 breathing. Feel your ribs move your hand.
- Switch to Kindness: Write down one phrase you'd say to a friend in the same situation. ("You had a tough day, but you're trying.")
- Read It Aloud: Speak the kind phrase out loud. Notice how it feels in your body.
- Choose One Step: Pick the next small action, drink some water, write two lines, step outside. Not a grand plan. A steadying step.
This practice blends physiology (breath) with narrative (self-talk), weakening the critic on both fronts.

## 5. The 15-Minute Embodied Reset (Chapter 5) - 15 minutes
App match: recovery menu, 15-min option

Use this when you feel scattered, heavy, or on edge. No equipment. No special clothing. Just a timer.
- Minute 0 to 3, Settle and breathe: Sit or stand tall. Place a hand on the belly. Inhale gently through the nose for four, exhale through the nose or mouth for six. Feel your ribs move your hand. Do five to eight rounds.
- Minute 3 to 8, Mindful walk: Walk a simple loop. Feel your feet. Name three sights, three sounds, and three sensations, then repeat. Keep your gaze soft. If the mind wanders, bring it back to your steps. You are not chasing calm. You are cultivating it.
- Minute 8 to 12, Micro-mobility: Choose one area that holds stress and give it four minutes. Neck rolls in slow arcs. Shoulder circles forward and back. Hip circles or gentle lunges. Move within comfort, never into sharp pain. Let each exhale soften the outer edge of effort.
- Minute 12 to 14, Body scan: Stand or sit. Scan head to toe. Forehead, jaw, throat, shoulders, chest, belly, hips, legs, feet. Label what you find: tight, warm, buzzy, calm. No judgment. Just inventory.
- Minute 14 to 15, Choose one next step: Ask: what is one small action that would help right now? Drink water. Send one email. Take a shower. Write two lines. Pick the smallest good step and do it. Let this be the hinge that turns the day.
Write down how you feel. Better is enough. If it didn't shift much, that is data. Try again later or make it shorter next time. The practice works because you show up, not because you force a result.

## 6. The Recovery Menu (Chapter 6)
App match: Gemini recovery-menu (named after this exercise)

Write your own menu of recovery options. Include both micro and macro choices. Keep it visible on your phone or desk.
Micro (under 10 minutes):
- Three deep breaths
- One stretch
- One glass of water
- Step outside for air
- Quick check-in with a friend
Macro (hours to days):
- A long walk or hike
- A weekend without email
- A hobby you savour
- A family game night
- A full day offline
Next time stress drains you, consult the menu instead of improvising. Choose one micro if time is short, one macro if time allows. Over time, you train the nervous system to expect, and accept, recovery as part of resilience.

## 7. The One-Point Focus Practice (Chapter 7)
App match: recovery menu, 1-min option

Choose one everyday object: a cup, a pen, a leaf.
Sit comfortably. Place the object where you can see it.
Spend two minutes observing. Notice colour, shape, texture. Trace its outline with your eyes.
When the mind wanders, and it will, gently return to the object. No judgment.
After two minutes, close your eyes and picture it in detail. See how much more you recall.
This practice trains the spotlight of attention. The object does not matter. The reps do. Each return is a strengthening.

## 8. The 90-Second Emotional Pause (Chapter 8)
App match: Theo 90-second-hinge AND Gemini wave-rider (two takes on one exercise, prime merge candidate)

Try this the next time a strong emotion hits:
- Stop. Close your eyes if you can.
- Name. Say silently, "This is anger." "This is fear." "This is sadness."
- Locate. Notice where in the body it sits, chest, throat, stomach, jaw.
- Breathe. Slow in-breath, longer out-breath. Ride the wave for 90 seconds.
- Reopen. Ask gently: "What is the next wise step?"
Many people find the initial emotional wave peaks and begins to ease within about 90 seconds if they don't fuel it, though this varies from person to person and emotion to emotion. This pause is designed to ride that curve rather than fight it.

## 9. The Mindful Listening Practice (Chapter 9)
App match: not yet prototyped (candidate for a future tool, needs two people so lower priority for a solo web app)

Try this with a partner, friend, or colleague.
Set a timer for three minutes.
Speaker talks, choosing any topic.
Listener's only job: pay full attention. No interruptions. No advice. Just presence.
When the timer ends, switch roles.
After both rounds, reflect: how did it feel to be heard? To listen fully?
This exercise builds the muscle of mindful connection, attention without agenda.
