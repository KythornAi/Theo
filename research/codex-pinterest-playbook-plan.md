# Pinterest Pin Playbook for Scribe

## 1. Strategic Foundation

### 1.1 Purpose of Pinterest for Paper N Pixels
- Pinterest is the Month-1 traffic channel.
- Primary goals:
  - Drive traffic to Etsy listings.
  - Drive traffic to blog posts.
  - Build early awareness before launch.
  - Validate pain-point messaging before products are live.
  - Collect data on which ADHD productivity angles get saves, clicks, and outbound traffic.

### 1.2 Brand Positioning
Paper N Pixels helps adults with ADHD reset their day without starting over.

Core differentiation:
- Browser-based ADHD planning tool, not another printable PDF.
- Designed for real ADHD behavior: overwhelm, avoidance, low energy, initiation friction, planner abandonment.
- Focuses on “what can I do next?” rather than perfect planning.

Competitor contrast:
- FutureADHD, Daily Focus Club, Manifestable: mostly PDF, GoodNotes, printable systems.
- PNP: digital, browser-based, flexible, forgiving, reset-friendly.

### 1.3 Target Audience
Adults with ADHD, primarily women ages 25-45.

Core struggles:
- Abandoning planners.
- Feeling behind by 10 a.m.
- Avoiding tasks because starting feels too big.
- Buying productivity tools, then not using them.
- Having energy that changes throughout the day.
- Needing structure but resisting rigid systems.

Emotional state while scrolling:
- Avoidant.
- Overwhelmed.
- Searching for relief.
- Tired of being told to “just plan better.”
- Drawn to pins that feel personally accurate.

### 1.4 Core Messaging Principle
The pin itself must prove that PNP understands ADHD.

A strong PNP pin should make the viewer think:

> “That is exactly what happens to me.”

Before it says:

> “Buy this planner.”

## 5. Pin Creation Workflow

### 5.1 Weekly Planning
Every week, Scribe should choose:

- 2 product angles.
- 2 blog angles.
- 1 lead magnet angle.
- 1 differentiation angle.
- 1 seasonal or trend angle.

Output target:
- 15 to 30 fresh pins per week.
- Multiple pins can point to the same URL if the creative and hook are meaningfully different.

### 5.2 Research Step
For each batch:

1. Choose the destination URL or future destination.
2. Identify the primary keyword.
3. Search Pinterest manually or with PinClicks.
4. Record related keywords.
5. Identify top pin patterns:
   - Headlines.
   - Colors.
   - Layouts.
   - Repeated phrases.
   - Visual formats.
6. Note what is missing from competitors.

Scribe should specifically look for:
- Pins that overpromise.
- Pins that feel too neurotypical.
- Pins that assume high energy.
- Pins that rely on pretty planner aesthetics but ignore initiation.

### 5.3 Hook Generation
For each destination, generate 10 to 20 hooks.

Hook categories:
- Pain point: “Your planner should not fall apart when your day does.”
- Contrarian: “Stop planning your whole day when you can’t start one task.”
- Specific method: “The 3-task reset for ADHD overwhelm.”
- Emotional recognition: “For the day you already feel behind.”
- Product promise: “A browser-based planner for ADHD reset days.”
- Curiosity: “The planning rule that works better than a full to-do list.”

Then choose:
- 3 save-focused hooks.
- 3 click-focused hooks.
- 3 buyer-intent hooks.

### 5.4 Design Step
For each pin:

- Use 1000x1500px as default.
- Use 1000x2100px for tutorial, checklist, or multi-step pins.
- Use large readable text.
- Keep headline to 4-9 words where possible.
- Use multiple screenshots or product mockups when relevant.
- Use real product screenshots once available.
- Avoid AI-generated lifestyle imagery.
- Use cohesive PNP colors, but vary backgrounds enough that the feed does not look repetitive.

Design must pass the “ADHD scroll test”:
- Can the main idea be understood in under 2 seconds?
- Does the headline name a real ADHD pain?
- Is there one clear visual focus?
- Is the text readable on mobile?
- Does it feel useful, not decorative?

### 5.5 Copy Step
For each pin, create:

- File name.
- Pin title.
- Pin description.
- Destination URL.
- Board.
- CTA.
- Keyword notes.
- Hypothesis being tested.

### 5.6 Publishing Step
Before publishing:

- Confirm file name is keyword-rich.
- Confirm title matches visual hook.
- Confirm description includes primary keyword naturally.
- Confirm destination URL is correct.
- Confirm board is relevant.
- Confirm no text is too small.
- Confirm no claim is too broad or medicalized.
- Confirm pin does not look like generic wellness content.

### 5.7 Tracking Step
After publishing, log the pin in Google Sheets.

Fields:
- Date published.
- Pin URL.
- Destination URL.
- Board.
- Product/blog/lead magnet category.
- Pin format.
- Hook.
- Primary keyword.
- Secondary keywords.
- Design template.
- CTA.
- Impressions.
- Saves.
- Pin clicks.
- Outbound clicks.
- CTR.
- Save rate.
- Conversion, when available.
- Notes.
- Next action.

## 6. Metrics and Iteration

### 6.1 Core Metrics
Track:

- Impressions: Pinterest distribution.
- Saves: usefulness and resonance.
- Pin clicks: interest in the pin.
- Outbound clicks: traffic quality.
- Outbound CTR: conversion from Pinterest to site/Etsy.
- Save rate: whether the pin is valuable.
- Etsy visits.
- Etsy favorites.
- Gumroad visits.
- Email signups.
- Sales, once live.

### 6.2 Interpretation Guide

High impressions, low clicks:
- Hook may be broad but not compelling.
- CTA may be weak.
- Design may be pretty but not urgent.
- Need clearer problem/solution.

Low impressions, high save rate:
- Good content, weak SEO or board match.
- Rework title, description, keywords.
- Create more variations.

High saves, low outbound clicks:
- Pin is useful but self-contained.
- Add stronger reason to click.
- Use “get the full reset” or “download the free page.”

High outbound clicks, low conversion:
- Destination mismatch.
- Etsy listing may need stronger first image.
- Blog intro may not match the pin promise.
- Product may need clearer value proposition.

Low everything:
- Hook too generic.
- Design not readable.
- Keyword too competitive.
- Wrong board.
- Not specific enough to ADHD.

### 6.3 Iteration Cadence
Weekly:
- Review pins from 7-14 days ago for early signals.
- Identify top hooks by save rate.
- Identify top keywords by impressions.
- Identify top formats by outbound CTR.

Monthly:
- Create 5 to 10 new variations from the best performers.
- Retire weak templates.
- Update board strategy.
- Compare product vs blog vs lead magnet traffic.
- Identify new content opportunities from search terms.

## 7. A/B Testing System

### 7.1 What to Test
Only test one major variable at a time.

Variables:
- Hook.
- Pin format.
- Color palette.
- Product screenshot vs lifestyle mockup.
- Pain-point headline vs solution headline.
- Short pin vs long pin.
- “ADHD planner” vs “Daily Reset.”
- “Browser-based” vs “digital.”
- “Executive dysfunction” vs simpler language like “can’t start.”

### 7.2 Test Structure
For each test:

- Same destination URL.
- Same board or closely related board.
- Same keyword family.
- 2 to 4 creative variants.
- Published within the same week.
- Tracked for at least 14-30 days.

### 7.3 Example A/B Tests

Test 1: Pain point vs product promise  
A: “Your planner should not fall apart when your day does.”  
B: “A browser-based ADHD planner for reset days.”

Test 2: Method vs emotion  
A: “The 3-task rule for ADHD overwhelm.”  
B: “For the day you already feel behind.”

Test 3: Differentiation  
A: “Not another printable you’ll forget exists.”  
B: “An ADHD planner that lives in your browser.”

Test 4: CTA  
A: “Read the reset method.”  
B: “Get the free ADHD reset page.”

### 7.4 Winning Criteria
A winner depends on goal:

For awareness:
- Impressions.
- Saves.
- Save rate.

For traffic:
- Outbound clicks.
- Outbound CTR.

For product:
- Etsy visits.
- Favorites.
- Add to cart.
- Sales.

For lead magnets:
- Email signup rate.

## 9. Common Mistakes in ADHD/Productivity Pins

### 9.1 Sounding Too Neurotypical
Avoid:
- “Just organize your day.”
- “Plan your perfect morning.”
- “Wake up at 5 a.m. and crush your goals.”
- “Stay consistent every day.”

Better:
- “Start from where you are.”
- “Pick the next doable task.”
- “Reset your day without rewriting everything.”

### 9.2 Selling Aesthetic Planning Instead of Functional Relief
The audience may like pretty tools, but they buy relief.

Avoid leading with:
- Beautiful pages.
- Cozy desk scenes.
- Perfect routines.

Lead with:
- Less overwhelm.
- Easier starting.
- Fewer abandoned planners.
- Low-energy options.
- Resetting after falling behind.

### 9.3 Overloading the Pin
ADHD viewers need fast comprehension.

Avoid:
- Tiny text.
- Too many fonts.
- Too many competing elements.
- Overly long headlines.
- Dense paragraphs on the image.

### 9.4 Using Shame-Based Productivity Language
Avoid:
- Lazy.
- Excuses.
- Discipline.
- No more procrastination forever.
- Fix your ADHD.

Better:
- Stuck.
- Overwhelmed.
- Low-energy.
- Reset.
- Start smaller.
- Reduce friction.

### 9.5 Making Medical Claims
Avoid:
- “Cures executive dysfunction.”
- “Fixes ADHD.”
- “Guaranteed to stop procrastination.”

Better:
- “Designed for ADHD planning struggles.”
- “Helps reduce planning friction.”
- “Supports task initiation.”

### 9.6 Hiding the Differentiation
PNP should not look like every printable planner brand.

Always consider whether to include:
- Browser-based.
- Reset tool.
- Not another printable.
- Built for planner abandonment.
- Low-energy mode.
