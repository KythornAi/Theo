# MNM Four-App Value-Add Audit — Second Pass

**Date:** 2026-06-06  
**Context:** Prior audit was too timid. This pass focuses on commercial/bundle-level positioning, not micro-features.

---

## Why the Previous Audit Was Too Small

The first audit treated these as **timer apps that need better outputs**. Every recommendation was flavoured polish: add a chooser chip here, a pattern sentence there, a better keepsake. The thinking was incremental — "how do we make the app slightly more useful when the user finishes?"

That framing misses the commercial problem.

**The real gap:** these apps currently feel like *chapter companions* — tools you might use once to follow the book. They do not feel like products you'd pay for independently or return to weekly. The timer/breathing exercise is the only reason to open them. If you skip that, there's nothing left.

A bundle buyer on Gumroad doesn't think "I want a timer for Chapter 8." They think "This looks like a useful thing I might actually use." The previous audit's recommendations would make the apps marginally better at what they already do. They wouldn't change what the apps *are*.

**What was too small:**

1. **"Better keepsakes"** — Still assumes the core value is completing the timed exercise. It isn't. Most people who buy a digital wellness bundle won't complete a timed exercise more than once or twice. The value has to exist *before and apart from* the exercise.

2. **"Add a chooser chip"** — This is a UX refinement, not a product. A "before-scan reason chooser" does not make Body Stress Scan worth paying for. It makes it marginally more personalised.

3. **"Quick Card mode"** — The only recommendation that touched real standalone value, but it was framed as a time-saver, not as the primary product. It should be inverted: the Reset Card IS the product. The audit is optional context.

4. **"Build your tiny recovery menu"** — Correct instinct, but buried under four other recommendations. This is the actual Chapter 6 exercise and the only reason Recovery Menu Hub exists as a standalone. It should be the first screen, not an optional section.

---

## What "Substantial Standalone Value" Actually Means

A paid bundle item must pass this test: **If someone never touches the timer or breathing exercise, would they still get something useful?**

Currently:
- Body Stress Scan: No. Without the scan, it's an intro page.
- Balance Audit: Borderline yes. The audit alone produces something, but most users won't complete 5+5 items.
- Recovery Menu Hub: No. It's a session launcher. No session = no value.
- 90-Second Pause: No. It's a guided breathing flow. Skip the breathing = nothing.

The fix isn't adding chips. It's repositioning what the app fundamentally does.

## App-by-App Critique and Replacement Recommendations

### 1. Body Stress Scan

**Why the previous recommendations were too small:**

"Before-scan reason chooser" and "carry it forward micro-choice" are nice-to-haves that make the existing flow more personalised. They don't address the core problem: if you skip the guided scan, you get nothing. The app is a wrapper for a 3-minute exercise.

"Plain-language pattern sentence" is flavour text. "Screenshot-friendly body summary card" is polish. None of these change what the product is.

**What the bigger product role should be:**

Body Stress Scan should be a **body tension awareness tool**, not a guided scan with a keepsake. The ebook's actual point (Chapter 1) is that stress shows up physically before you notice it mentally. The app should help you notice that pattern over time — but without dashboards, history, or tracking.

The repositioning: make the scan *optional*. The primary value is a simple, repeatable **"where am I holding tension right now?"** check-in that takes 15 seconds. The full guided scan is the deeper dive for when you have time.

**Substantial but still lightweight upgrades:**

1. **15-second tension check (primary mode):** One screen. A body silhouette. Tap where you feel tight right now. Get a one-line sentence: "Today tension is in your jaw and shoulders." Copyable. Done. This is the daily-use product. The full scan is "Slow version" — accessible but not the default.

2. **Body tension vocabulary card:** A static reference card showing the seven areas and common sensation words (tight, buzzing, numb, warm, heavy, sharp, dull). Downloadable PNG or on-screen reference. Useful even if you never run the scan. Positions the app as a learning tool, not just a timer wrapper.

3. **"What I'm noticing" freeform note field on the 15-second check:** One optional line the user can write. Gets included in the copyable keepsake. Turns the output from "you tapped jaw and shoulders" into something personal enough to paste into a journal.

**What to avoid:**
- No body-map history or heatmaps. Dashboard creep.
- No "common patterns for you" language. Requires tracking.
- No "stress type" or nervous system state interpretations. Claims territory.

### 2. Balance Audit Reset Card

**Why the previous recommendations were too small:**

"Quick Card mode" was the right instinct but wrong framing. It positioned the quick path as a time-saver for busy users. It should be the **primary product**. The full 5+5 audit is the deep version.

"Card personalisation line" and "two export layouts" are polish. They make a good card better but don't change what the product is.

"Balance audit prompt bank" is busywork. Users don't want more prompts — they want to get to the card faster.

**What the bigger product role should be:**

Balance Audit Reset Card should be a **pocket reset card maker**, not an audit tool with a card attached. The ebook's actual point (Chapter 2) is that you need something small and visible to reach for when things tilt. The audit is context for making a better card — but the card is the product.

The repositioning: start with the card. The audit is "Build a better card" — a deeper optional path.

**Substantial but still lightweight upgrades:**

1. **Card-first flow (new default):** First screen asks for one tilter and one steadier. Generates a card immediately. "Make it better" button unlocks the full 5+5 audit for users who want richer input. This inverts the current structure: card is immediate, audit is optional depth.

2. **Three card templates:** Same content, three visual treatments — "Phone lockscreen" (current), "Index card" (landscape, for printing), and "Desktop sticky" (smaller, horizontal). Users pick which they want. Different use cases feel like different products. Currently both exports are the same content in two sizes — that's a format choice, not a product choice.

3. **"When [tilter], reach for [steadier]" as the card's headline:** Currently the card is "Three breaths / Name the tilt / One tiny steadying action" — generic advice that works for anyone. That's fine, but it's not *your* card. The personal pair should be the headline. The three-step instructions become supporting text.

**What to avoid:**
- No "Saved cards" library. Dashboard creep.
- No weekly review prompts or adjustment tracking. Planner vibes.
- No "recommended steadiers for your tilters" AI suggestions. Claims territory.

### 3. Recovery Menu Hub

**Why the previous recommendations were too small:**

"Build your tiny recovery menu" was the correct recommendation — it's literally the Chapter 6 exercise — but it was listed as item #1 of 4 recommendations. It should be the **entire product repositioning**.

"What kind of recovery do I need chooser" is UX guidance for the existing session picker. It doesn't add standalone value.

"Session-specific exit card" is keepsake polish.

"No-timer recovery list" was smart — it preserves value when the user skips the sessions — but it was listed last and framed as a fallback.

**What the bigger product role should be:**

Recovery Menu Hub should be a **personal recovery menu builder with optional guided sessions**, not a session launcher. The ebook's actual point (Chapter 6) is that recovery choices should be visible so you don't improvise while drained. The timed sessions are implementations of that principle — but the menu is the product.

The repositioning: first screen is "Build your menu." Timed sessions are "Practice one now" — accessible but secondary.

**Substantial but still lightweight upgrades:**

1. **Menu builder as the primary mode:** First screen: "What helps you recover?" User picks or writes 3-5 micro options (under 10 minutes) and 1-2 macro options (hours/days). Output: a copyable/screenshotable personal recovery menu card. The four timed sessions are "Practice one of these" — available from the menu card, not the other way around.

2. **"What's available right now?" quick filter:** After building a menu, user can tap "I have 2 minutes" or "I have an hour" to see which of their own options fit. Not a recommendation engine — just a filter on their own list. Makes the menu feel actionable.

3. **Tiny recovery prompt deck:** A set of 12-16 tiny recovery ideas from the book (water, stretch, step outside, one breath, close one tab, send later, phone in another room). Presented as a swipeable deck on the build screen. User can tap to add any to their personal menu. Gives them a starting point without inventing from blank.

**What to avoid:**
- No recovery frequency tracking or "how often do you actually do these" analytics.
- No push notifications or reminders to recover.
- No "recommended for you" suggestions based on usage.

### 4. 90-Second Emotional Pause

**Why the previous recommendations were too small:**

"Fast name-and-locate only path" was the right instinct — preserve value when the user skips the 90-second breathing. But it was framed as a fallback for "real strong-emotion moments when the timer feels like too much." That's correct psychologically but wrong commercially.

"Emotion word ladder" is vocabulary polish.

"Next-wise-step chips" and "wave check-in" are keepsake improvements.

**What the bigger product role should be:**

90-Second Emotional Pause should be an **emotional naming and locating tool with optional breath support**, not a breathing exercise with a naming step. The ebook's actual point (Chapter 8) is that naming and locating interrupts the emotional loop. The 90-second breathing is one way to ride the wave — but the naming and locating is the skill.

The repositioning: make "Name and Locate" the primary product. The 90-second breathing is "Stay with it longer" — available but optional.

**Substantial but still lightweight upgrades:**

1. **Name and Locate as the primary mode:** First screen: "What's the feeling?" (tap or type) → "Where is it in your body?" (tap silhouette) → "What do you want to do with this?" (next wise step chips). Done. Total time: 30 seconds. Output: a copyable emotional check-in. The 90-second breathing is "Stay with the wave" — accessible from the output screen for users who want it.

2. **Emotion vocabulary reference card:** A static downloadable card showing the 10 emotion chips plus 2-3 specificity words under each (anger → irritated, resentful, protective; sadness → disappointed, lonely, tender). Educational value even if you never run the pause. Positions the app as a naming skill tool, not just a breathing timer.

3. **"Wise step" as the persistent output, not the breathing duration:** Currently the keepsake says "What I noticed in the breath: 1 min 23 s of slow breathing." That's timing data, not value. The persistent output should be: "What I felt / Where it sat / What I'll do next." The breathing duration is optional detail. The wise step is the actionable part.

**What to avoid:**
- No emotion history or "your patterns over time" analysis.
- No intensity ratings (1-10) or before/after scoring.
- No "what usually helps with [emotion]" suggestions. Coaching territory.

## Top-8 Decision List for Kyle

Prioritised by commercial value lift, not implementation effort.

1. **Recovery Menu Hub: Menu builder as primary mode.** This is the biggest repositioning. Currently a session launcher; should be a personal menu builder. The four sessions become "practice one of these."

2. **Balance Audit: Card-first flow.** Invert the structure. Card is immediate; audit is optional depth. Users arrive, make a card in 30 seconds, leave with something useful.

3. **90-Second Pause: Name-and-Locate as primary mode.** The breathing is optional. The naming and locating is the skill. 30 seconds to a useful output.

4. **Body Stress Scan: 15-second tension check as primary mode.** Full guided scan becomes "Slow version." Daily-use product is tap-where-you're-tight, get a sentence, done.

5. **Balance Audit: "When [tilter], reach for [steadier]" as card headline.** Makes the card personal, not generic advice. The three-step instructions become supporting text.

6. **Recovery Menu Hub: Tiny recovery prompt deck.** Gives users starting material for building their menu. Swipeable, tappable-to-add.

7. **90-Second Pause: Emotion vocabulary reference card.** Static, downloadable, educational. Value even without running the pause.

8. **Body Stress Scan: Body tension vocabulary card.** Static, downloadable. Positions the app as a learning tool.

## What This Pass Deliberately Did NOT Recommend

- **No keepsake polish.** Keepsakes improve with better primary modes. Don't polish the output of a flow that shouldn't be the default.
- **No chooser chips on existing flows.** The flows themselves need repositioning, not more personalisation on top.
- **No "quick mode" framing.** Quick isn't a mode — it's the product. Deep is the optional mode.
- **No cross-app consistency work.** Get the individual products right first. Consistency is polish.
- **No implementation details.** This is product strategy, not a build spec.
